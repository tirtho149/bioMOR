Transformer models for transcriptomic analysis process thousands of genes or pathways, although only a subset requires deep computation. Mixture-of-Recursions (MoR) addresses this inefficiency through adaptive token-choice or expert-choice routing, but its routers are \emph{biology-agnostic}, relying primarily on individual token representations. We propose \textbf{bioMoR}, a biology-guided recursive Transformer that injects a learned gene--gene or pathway--pathway interaction graph into an MoR backbone through three mechanisms. The graph refines a compact set of interpretable marker or pathway tokens, biases self-attention toward interacting tokens, and guides depth allocation through a zero-initialized graph-convolutional router. Consequently, biologically influential tokens receive deeper computation, while less relevant tokens are frozen at shallower recursion depths. bioMoR supports both expert-choice and token-choice routing and reuses a single weight-shared block for parameter efficiency. Across eight single-cell and six multi-omics benchmarks under a unified five-fold cross-validation protocol, bioMoR improves average macro-F1 from $67.1\%$ to $73.5\%$ over the strongest biology-agnostic MoR baseline, while using $75\%$ fewer parameters and up to $58\%$ fewer FLOPs. The resulting token-specific recursion depths additionally provide an interpretable measure of both token-level importance and allocated computation.

Recent advances in high-throughput sequencing technologies have enabled the measurement of thousands of molecular features at single-cell and multi-omics resolution, providing unprecedented opportunities to study cellular heterogeneity, disease mechanisms, and precision medicine \cite{luecken2019current,stuart2019integrative,hasin2017multiomics}. Transformer-based architectures have recently become a powerful paradigm for modeling these high-dimensional genomic data because self-attention can capture long-range dependencies among genes and pathways. Foundation models such as Geneformer, scBERT, scGPT, and scFoundation have demonstrated remarkable performance across a wide range of transcriptomic tasks, including cell-type annotation, perturbation prediction, and representation learning \cite{theodoris2023transfer,scbert2022,cui2024scgpt,hao2024large}.

Despite these advances, conventional transformers remain computationally expensive for genomic data. Self-attention scales quadratically with the number of input tokens, while stacking multiple transformer layers further increases memory and parameter complexity \cite{vaswani2017attention}. More importantly, existing transformer architectures allocate the same amount of computation to every gene or pathway, although only a small subset is typically informative for a specific biological condition, cell type, or disease phenotype. This uniform computation is inconsistent with the sparse and hierarchical nature of biological systems.

Recent work has explored two complementary directions to improve transformer efficiency. Efficient-attention methods, including Linformer, Performer, and Nyströmformer, reduce the computational complexity of self-attention through low-rank or kernel approximations \cite{wang2020linformer,choromanski2021performer,xiong2021nystromformer}. Separately, adaptive computation methods, including Adaptive Computation Time, Mixture-of-Experts, Mixture-of-Depths, and the recently proposed Mixture-of-Recursions (MoR), allocate different amounts of computation to different inputs by dynamically routing tokens through recursive transformer blocks \cite{graves2016act,shazeer2017moe,raposo2024mixture,bae2025mixture}. Although these approaches substantially improve computational efficiency, their routing decisions are entirely data-driven and do not exploit well-established biological relationships among genes or pathways.

In parallel, numerous studies have demonstrated the value of incorporating biological priors into deep learning models. Marker-gene databases such as PanglaoDB and CellMarker organize experimentally validated cell-type markers \cite{franzen2019panglaodb,hu2023cellmarker}, while pathway databases such as Reactome provide curated gene--pathway and pathway--pathway relationships \cite{gillespie2022reactome}. Biology-aware architectures, including pathway-informed networks, Genomap, and GenoHoption, further show that integrating prior biological knowledge improves interpretability and predictive performance \cite{elmarakeby2021biologically,islam2023cartography,cheng2024genohoption}. However, existing approaches use biological knowledge primarily for feature construction or network design rather than for determining how computational resources should be allocated during inference.

In this work, we introduce \textbf{bioMoR} (\textbf{bio}logy-guided \textbf{M}ixture-\textbf{o}f-\textbf{R}ecursions), a biology-guided adaptive recursive transformer for transcriptomic learning. Unlike existing recursive transformers, bioMoR incorporates gene--gene or Reactome pathway--pathway interaction knowledge into both representation learning and adaptive routing. Biological interaction networks first refine gene or pathway embeddings through graph-based propagation, and the resulting biological prior is subsequently combined with the data-driven routing score to determine which marker tokens receive deeper recursive computation. Consequently, bioMoR allocates computation according to both the observed sample and its underlying biological context. The proposed framework naturally supports both expert-choice and token-choice routing while preserving the parameter efficiency of shared recursive transformer blocks.

We evaluate bioMoR on eight single-cell transcriptomic datasets and six multi-omics benchmarks (fourteen in total) under a unified 5-fold cross-validation protocol. On average, bioMoR outperforms Vanilla Transformers, Recursive Transformers, and biology-agnostic Mixture-of-Recursions under both expert-choice and token-choice routing: its best configuration reaches an average macro-F1 of \textbf{73.5\%}, improving over Vanilla Transformers (66.2\%), Recursive Transformers (66.4\%), and MoR (67.1\%) by roughly $6$--$7$ points, while using approximately $4\times$ fewer transformer parameters and reducing recursive FLOPs by up to 58\%. A controlled analysis further isolates \emph{where} biology helps: a rigid hand-built prior injected into the router fails, whereas a \emph{learned} interaction graph is effective through three mechanisms---smoothing token embeddings, biasing self-attention toward graph-adjacent tokens, and steering depth allocation through a zero-initialised graph-convolution router---and combining all three is best.

Our contributions are summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose \textbf{bioMoR}, a biology-guided adaptive recursive transformer that unifies marker/pathway token compression, weight-shared recursion, and adaptive per-token depth for both single-cell and multi-omics data.

    \item We introduce a \textbf{Bio Router} together with a \emph{learned} low-rank gene--gene graph that smooths the input before token selection, and study both carefully with degree-matched random-graph controls and warm-start stress tests.

    \item Through this controlled study we identify a \emph{learned} interaction graph---not a fixed biological prior---as the decisive driver, and show it helps at \emph{both} the embedding and routing sites, an honest negative-and-positive result that clarifies where biological structure helps.

    \item On fourteen genomic benchmarks under a unified 5-fold cross-validation protocol, bioMoR improves average predictive performance over Vanilla Transformers, Recursive Transformers, and biology-agnostic MoR while substantially reducing parameters and FLOPs, and we report an honest comparison against strong classical baselines.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Positioning against recent biology-aware models.}
bioMoR sits among a fast-growing set of pathway- and graph-aware genomic models
(Table~\ref{tab:positioning}). Closest on the multi-omics side is PATH
\cite{howlader2026graph}, which conditions gene embeddings on patient-specific
alterations and refines a pathway graph inside attention on pathway-token cohorts;
Pathformer \cite{pathformer2023} likewise injects pathway structure into attention for
multi-omics classification. On the single-cell side, scBiGNN \cite{scbignn2023} learns
gene--gene structure through a bilevel graph network, and ORBIT \cite{orbit2026} operates
on pathway/program tokens with an asymmetric objective, while GeneMamba
\cite{genemamba2024} pursues efficiency through a linear-time state-space backbone rather
than adaptive recursion. Two points distinguish our work. First, unlike these models
bioMoR makes computation \emph{adaptive per token} through weight-shared recursion, so its
parameter/FLOP efficiency and its interpretable depth signal arise from the same
mechanism. Second, our controls speak directly to a recent cautionary literature: whereas
DOGMA \cite{dogma2024} emphasizes deterministic prior-driven structure, recent
re-evaluations \cite{pathwayprior2025} argue that sparsity---not biological
correctness---explains much of the benefit attributed to pathway priors. Our
degree-matched random-graph controls, and our finding that a \emph{learned} graph beats a
fixed biological one, corroborate that view within an adaptive-recursion setting. We run scBiGNN
head-to-head (Table~\ref{tab:baselines}) and position bioMoR against PATH along these axes; we do
not retrain PATH here, as it requires named-gene inputs the anonymised genomap features do not expose.

\section{Method}

\paragraph{Token interface (markers and pathways).}
bioMoR turns the input into $M\ll N$ interpretable tokens before any quadratic
attention. For single-cell expression these are \emph{learned marker} tokens from the
cross-attention router. For bulk multi-omics they are fixed \emph{Reactome pathway}
tokens: a sparse gene$\to$pathway membership pools each pathway's per-gene channels
(mean pooling for dense assays such as copy-number and expression; burden/sum pooling
for sparse binary mutation), so every token is a named pathway. Both feed the same
recursive stack. On this shared backbone the experiments answer three questions in turn:
(i)~whether weight-shared \emph{adaptive} recursion preserves accuracy while removing
parameters and FLOPs --- read as a ladder from a vanilla transformer to expert- and
token-choice Mixture-of-Recursions (Table~\ref{tab:main_cv5}); (ii)~how the biological
interaction graph should be \emph{formed} --- learned end-to-end versus a fixed hand-built
prior or a degree-matched random control; and (iii)~\emph{where} it should enter --- the
token embedding, the depth router, the attention bias, or all jointly (Fig.~\ref{fig:injection}). Each component below
is introduced so that these three ablations map one-to-one onto its design choice; the
remaining knobs (marker budget $M$ and the weight-sharing schedule) are held at the defaults
stated below and swept only in the supplementary material.


\subsection{Overview}
Let $x \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$ be the expression vector of a cell over $N$ genes. bioMoR
maps $x$ to cell-type logits through five stages: gene embedding, learnable marker
selection, marker-anchored compression, biology-informed recursive shared
transformation with marker refinement, and a pooled classifier
(Figure~\ref{fig:overview}).

\subsection{Gene Embedding}
Each gene $i$ is embedded as the sum of a learned identity vector
$\mathbf{e}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and a projection of its scalar expression,
$\mathbf{t}_i = \mathbf{e}_i + \mathbf{W}_v\, x_i$, following the gene-plus-value
scheme of \cite{cui2024scgpt,theodoris2023transfer}. This is linear in $N$ and runs
over all genes before any compression. For multi-omics inputs a
gene carries $C$ aligned channels (mutation, copy-number, expression), $x_i\in\mathbb{R}^{C}$, and
the value projection $\mathbf{W}_v\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times C}$ fuses them at the token, so one
embedding path serves both the single-cell ($C{=}1$) and the multi-omics regimes evaluated in
\S\ref{sec:main} (PM, PC and 3M use $C{=}2,1,3$ before pathway pooling).

\subsection{Cross-Attention Marker Router}
We identify markers with a cross-attention \emph{router}. We maintain $M$ learnable
marker queries $\mathbf{q}_m \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$; each attends over the genes through a
shared key projection $\mathbf{k}_i = \mathbf{W}_k \mathbf{e}_i$, giving selection
weights
$\mathbf{w}_m = \mathrm{softmax}\big(\mathbf{q}_m \mathbf{K}^{\top}/(\tau\sqrt{d})\big)$
over \emph{all} $N$ genes, with temperature $\tau$ annealed from soft to peaked.
Because the softmax spans all genes, gradients reach every gene, so a query can
migrate to an informative gene it did not initially favour, the property hard top-$k$
routing lacks. Two ingredients are essential: the all-gene softmax, and a
\emph{peaked initialisation} that points each query at a distinct gene's key, so
training starts at random-selection quality rather than a uniform average of all
genes. At inference each query collapses to its arg-max gene
$g_m = \arg\max_i \mathbf{q}_m^{\top}\mathbf{k}_i$, giving discrete, interpretable
markers and $\mathcal{O}(M^2 d)$ attention. The interface also admits a Concrete
selector \cite{balin2019concrete,jang2017categorical} and fixed variance- or random-selected
panels as drop-in replacements; the cross-attention router is our default throughout.

\subsection{Marker Tokens}
Each query produces one marker token combining the (soft-)selected gene identity and
expression,
$\mathbf{c}_m = (\mathbf{w}_m^{\top}\mathbf{E}) + \mathbf{W}_v\,(\mathbf{w}_m^{\top}\mathbf{x})$,
where $\mathbf{E}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times d}$ are the gene-identity embeddings. The two
contributions are placed on a common scale by the pre-norm LayerNorm at the entry of
the shared block (Sec.~\ref{sec:rec}).

\subsection{Weight-Shared Adaptive Recursion (from MoR)}
\label{sec:rec}
We reuse the Mixture-of-Recursions backbone \cite{bae2025mixture}: a \emph{single} pre-norm
block $f_\theta$ is applied up to $K$ times, $\mathbf{H}^{(t+1)}=f_\theta(\mathbf{H}^{(t)})$,
$\mathbf{H}^{(0)}=\mathbf{C}$, tying all depth-wise parameters
\cite{dehghani2019universal,lan2020albert} so the stack's size is independent of $K$. Depth
is \emph{adaptive per token} (visualised in the supplementary material): \emph{expert-choice} keeps the
top-$\lceil c_t M\rceil$ tokens each step (our headline) and \emph{token-choice} lets each
token self-gate with a Switch-style load-balancing loss \cite{shazeer2017outrageously}; both
share $f_\theta$ and reduce to fixed-depth recursion when routing is off. A token's
surviving-step count is its interpretable \emph{recursion depth} $d_m\in\{0,\dots,K\}$---an
architecture-intrinsic compute-allocation score. A per-token gate recomputed from the updated
embeddings, $g^{(t)}_m=\sigma(\mathrm{MLP}_r(\mathbf{H}^{(t)}_m))$, refines markers between
steps.

\paragraph{The architecture ladder.}
These choices define the four rungs compared in Table~\ref{tab:main_cv5}. \emph{Vanilla} is $K$
independent blocks with no adaptive routing; \emph{Recursive} ties them into one shared block at
fixed depth; biology-agnostic \emph{MoR} adds expert- or token-choice adaptive depth on that
shared block; and \emph{bioMoR} augments MoR with the learned interaction graph of
\S\ref{sec:biorouter}. Every rung above Vanilla uses a single shared block, so its parameter count
is independent of $K$ --- the $4\times$ reduction at $K{=}4$ read off in Table~\ref{tab:main_cv5}.

\subsection{Biology-Informed Routing}
\label{sec:biorouter}
The backbone so far---embedding, marker/pathway compression, weight-shared recursion, and
expert-/token-choice depth routing---follows Mixture-of-Recursions and recursive transformers
\cite{bae2025mixture,dehghani2019universal,lan2020albert}. bioMoR's contribution is to inject
a \emph{learned} biological interaction graph at \emph{two} sites of this backbone: the depth
\emph{router} and the token \emph{embedding}. At the router, the expert-choice depth logit for
token $m$ at step $t$ takes the form
\begin{equation}
\tilde r^{(t)}_m \;=\; \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{\tau}\,\mathbf{w}_r^{\top}\mathbf{H}^{(t)}_m}_{\text{data-driven (learned)}}
\;+\; \underbrace{\beta_t\,\pi_m}_{\text{biological prior}},
\label{eq:biorouter}
\end{equation}
and the keep/drop top-$\lceil c_t M\rceil$ and the gate
$g^{(t)}_m=\sigma(\tilde r^{(t)}_m)$ run exactly as before, so the recursion-depth
definition is unchanged but now reflects prior and data together. This is the same
loss-free additive-bias slot used by Switch routing \cite{shazeer2017outrageously},
except the bias is a per-gene \emph{biological} score, not a load-balancing scalar.

\paragraph{Site 1 (router): a zero-initialised graph-convolution residual.}
A first attempt sets $\pi_m$ to a fixed network centrality (z-scored eigenvector centrality
of the label-free co-expression / Reactome graph) and adds $\beta_t\pi_m$ in
Eq.~\ref{eq:biorouter}. This is a \emph{negative} result: the fixed prior does not separate
from a degree-matched random control and can pin the router to a bad operating point
(Sec.~\ref{sec:interaction}). We therefore replace it with a \emph{learned} residual---the
router message-passes over the row-normalised token graph $\mathbf{A}$ (below) and adds
$\sigma(\gamma_t)\,\mathrm{MLP}\big([\mathbf{A}\mathbf{H}^{(t)},\,\mathbf{H}^{(t)}-\mathbf{A}\mathbf{H}^{(t)}]\big)_m$
to the depth logit, with the MLP output \emph{zero-initialised} so the term begins as a
no-op and can only learn to help, never collapsing routing the way the fixed prior does.

\paragraph{The shared learned graph and Site 2 (embedding).}
All three mechanisms read one \emph{learned} low-rank graph. We attach an embedding
$\mathbf{E}\in\mathbb{R}^{M\times r}$ ($r{=}16$) whose row-cosine similarity is the affinity
$\mathbf{A}=\tilde{\mathbf{E}}\tilde{\mathbf{E}}^{\top}$,
$\tilde{\mathbf{E}}=\mathrm{normalize}(\mathbf{E})$, warm-started from the co-expression graph
(single cell) or the \emph{provided} Reactome pathway$\to$pathway adjacency
(multi-omics) and then refined end-to-end so it is
discriminative, not merely centrality-maximal. At the embedding site we smooth the tokens
along it, $\mathbf{c}\leftarrow(1-\lambda)\mathbf{c}+\lambda\,(\mathbf{c}\tilde{\mathbf{E}})\tilde{\mathbf{E}}^{\top}$
(learned $\lambda$, low-rank $\mathcal{O}(Mr)$), so its $r$ latent factors act as gene/pathway
programs that denoise each token toward its program consensus.

\paragraph{Site 3 (attention bias).}
The same learned graph finally biases the recursive block's self-attention: graph-adjacent
token pairs receive a fixed additive logit boost
$\lambda_{\mathrm{attn}}\,\mathbf{1}[A_{mn}{>}0]$ ($\lambda_{\mathrm{attn}}{=}2$), so each token
attends more strongly to its interaction neighbours during refinement. Unlike the
zero-initialised router (Site~1), this is a \emph{fixed}-strength term, so we validate its
contribution empirically rather than through the no-op argument of Thm.~2. Injecting the graph
through all three mechanisms---embedding smoothing (E), the depth router (R), and the attention
bias (A)---is bioMoR (ERA); the mechanism ablation (Fig.~\ref{fig:injection}) shows the
embedding pathway carries most of the gain, with the router and attention bias contributing
in combination (ERA best on Baron).

\paragraph{Complexity.}
Only the embedding and the marker router touch all $N$ genes ($\mathcal{O}(Nd)$, and an
$\mathcal{O}(MNd)$ cross-attention whose $M$ queries attend over $N$ keys); everything after
compression --- the recursive stack, the low-rank graph ($\mathcal{O}(Mr)$), pooling and head ---
runs on the $M\ll N$ tokens at $\mathcal{O}(M^2 d)$ per pass. Weight sharing removes the depth
dependence of the parameter count, and adaptive depth pays the $\mathcal{O}(M^2 d)$ recursion only
on surviving tokens; together these produce the parameter and FLOP savings quantified in
\S\ref{sec:efficiency}.

\subsection{Training Objective}
We minimise a composite loss
$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}} + \lambda\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{marker}}
+ \gamma\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{div}} + \zeta\mathcal{L}_{z} + \eta\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bal}}$,
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}}$ is class-weighted cross-entropy (inverse-frequency
weights on the train split); $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{marker}}$ is the cross-entropy of
an auxiliary linear classifier fed only the pre-recursion cluster tokens, forcing the
marker head to select task-sufficient genes;
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{div}}$ is the off-diagonal energy of the normalised marker Gram
matrix (prevents marker collapse); $\mathcal{L}_{z}$ is the router logit $z$-loss; and
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bal}}$ is the Switch load-balancing term
\cite{shazeer2017outrageously} for token-choice routing (expert-choice is balanced by
construction). Unless noted $\lambda{=}0.1$, $\gamma{=}0.05$, $\zeta{=}10^{-3}$,
$\eta{=}0.1$; $\tau$ anneals geometrically from $1$ to $0.1$ and each router query is
peak-initialised.

\section{Theoretical Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}
We show the graph-injection mechanisms are principled rather than heuristic: the embedding-site graph
is a \emph{provably optimal denoiser} (Thm.~1), the router-site graph is \emph{monotone-safe}
(Thm.~2), and the full combination---embedding, router, and attention bias (ERA)---dominates every
sub-combination (Cor.~1), the population-risk statement of the empirical ordering in
Fig.~\ref{fig:injection}. The attention bias enters Cor.~1 through the same no-op containment
($\lambda_{\mathrm{attn}}{=}0$); unlike the zero-initialised router it is deployed at fixed strength,
so it shares Cor.~1's population-optimum guarantee but not Thm.~2's zero-init training-trajectory one.
Full proofs are in the supplementary material.

\paragraph{Setup and assumptions.}
Model gene expression as signal-plus-noise $x=z+\varepsilon$ under three standing assumptions:
\textbf{(A1)} the noise is zero-mean, isotropic, and independent of the signal
($\varepsilon\sim(0,\sigma^2 I)$, $\varepsilon\perp z$, $\mathbb{E}z=0$); \textbf{(A2)} the signal
is \emph{low-rank}, $\Sigma=\mathbb{E}[zz^\top]=\sum_k s_k\,u_k u_k^\top$ with $s_1\ge s_2\ge\cdots$
and energy concentrated in a top-$r$ subspace; \textbf{(A3)} the head that reads the selection
statistics is $L_\Phi$-Lipschitz (used only to transfer the denoising bound to task risk,
Prop.~2 supp.). The learned-graph propagation of Sec.~\ref{sec:biorouter} is the linear filter
$T_\lambda=(1-\lambda)I+\lambda A$ with $A=\tilde E\tilde E^\top\succeq0$ of $\mathrm{rank}(A)\le r$
(at the router site $A$ is additionally taken row-stochastic, so $\Phi{=}0$ is an exact no-op);
we control the reconstruction error $R(T)=\mathbb{E}\lVert Tx-z\rVert^2$ of the signal fed to
marker selection. Three regimes nest by feasible set: $B_0$ (no propagation, $T{=}I$)
$\subseteq B_1$ (scalar shrink, $A{=}0$) $\subseteq B_2$ (learned graph). (A1)--(A2) are the standard
signal-plus-noise/spiked-covariance assumptions, and the low-rank $A$ mirrors the $\mathcal{O}(Mr)$
factorisation the model actually learns.

\paragraph{Theorem~1 (Denoising dominance).}
\emph{$R^\star(B_0)\ge R^\star(B_1)\ge R^\star(B_2)$, and}
\begin{equation*}
R^\star(B_0)-R^\star(B_2)\;\ge\;\sum_{i\le r}\frac{s_i^2}{s_i+\sigma^2}\;>\;0,
\end{equation*}
\emph{the optimum being the rank-$r$ Wiener filter $f_i^\star=s_i/(s_i+\sigma^2)$ on $v_i=u_i$.}

The gap is largest on high-variance biological modes ($s_i\gg\sigma^2$) and vanishes on pure
noise --- denoising helps exactly where structured signal lives --- and a Lipschitz-head
argument (supplementary) turns it into a strict reduction of an upper bound on the task risk.

\paragraph{Theorem~2 (Router monotone-safety).}
\emph{Let $A$ be row-stochastic and let the router augment its logit by $\Phi_t(AH)$ with $\Phi$
initialised at $0$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:biorouter}, Site~1). Then (i) the biology-augmented hypothesis
class contains the baseline, $\Theta_0\subset\Theta_G$, so its population optimum is no worse,
$R^\star(\Theta_G)\le R^\star(\Theta_0)$; (ii) gradient flow initialised at $\Phi{=}0$ starts from
the exact baseline loss and is non-increasing in the training objective; and (iii) the inequality
is strict whenever a token's optimal depth depends on its biological neighbourhood $(AH)_m$ rather
than on its own features $h_m$ alone (two tokens with equal $h_m$ but different $(AH)_m$ that a
baseline $\sigma(h_m)$ router cannot separate).}

Scope: (i)--(ii) are a \emph{no-harm} guarantee (population optimum and training trajectory from the
zero-init), not a promise of strict \emph{test}-risk improvement at finite sample --- so we treat the
theorem as licensing the augmentation and verify the gain against degree-matched random-graph controls
(Sec.~\ref{sec:interaction}). It is exactly the property the earlier \emph{fixed} centrality prior
lacked: a rank-one bias $\beta c$ initialised at $\beta_0\!\approx\!1$ neither starts at the no-op nor
expresses a non-collinear correction, so it can strictly raise risk and collapse (equal-centrality
hubs driven to identical depth) --- the theoretical cause of the fixed-prior failure reported
in Sec.~\ref{sec:interaction}.

A low-rank filter can also \emph{over-smooth}: $T_\lambda$ has eigenvalues $1-\lambda+\lambda a_k$, so
$\lambda\to1$ erases the low-variance modes that most often carry \emph{rare} populations. bioMoR
stays off this regime by \emph{learning} a bounded $\lambda$ (init $0.3$, keeping $1-\lambda>0$) and
by propagating over $M{\ll}N$ tokens rather than cells; the spectral and rare-cell analysis is in the
supplementary material.

\paragraph{Corollary~1 (All three mechanisms are best).}
\emph{With $R^\star_{\mathrm{all}},R^\star_{\mathrm{emb}},R^\star_{\mathrm{rt}},R^\star_{\mathrm{attn}},R^\star_{\mathrm{none}}$
the optima of the joint (ERA), embedding-only, router-only, attention-bias-only, and no-biology families,}
\begin{equation*}
R^\star_{\mathrm{all}}\;\le\;\min\{R^\star_{\mathrm{emb}},\,R^\star_{\mathrm{rt}},\,R^\star_{\mathrm{attn}}\}\;\le\;R^\star_{\mathrm{none}}.
\end{equation*}
Each mechanism admits a no-op---embedding $\lambda{=}0$, router $\Phi{=}0$, attention bias
$\lambda_{\mathrm{attn}}{=}0$---so switching any subset off recovers the corresponding restricted
family. The joint (ERA) family therefore contains every sub-family and its optimum dominates all of
them --- the population-risk form of the \emph{embedding, router, and attention bias combine (ERA
best)} ordering in Fig.~\ref{fig:injection}.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
We evaluate on \textbf{fourteen datasets}: eight genomap single-cell scRNA-seq suites
\cite{islam2023cartography} (Baron, Lung, Muraro, Oesophagus, Segerstolpe, Spleen, T-cell,
Xin) and six multi-omics cohorts --- three Reactome cohorts --- prostate, bladder (BLCA) and stomach (STAD) cancer --- that
fuse mutation, copy-number and expression through fixed pathway tokens, and three pan-cancer
TCGA settings of a binary primary-vs-metastatic task (\textbf{PM}, pan-cancer mutation$+$copy-number; \textbf{PC}, pan-cancer expression; \textbf{3M}, pan-cancer tri-modal --- all three modalities). All models share one unified \textbf{5-fold
cross-validation} protocol (stratified, seed~42; 20\% test, 10\%-of-train validation for early
stopping; per-gene $z$-scoring fit on the training fold only). The five folds are
\emph{identical across every variant}, so all comparisons are paired; we report macro-F1
(mean$\pm$SD over folds) and take the headline \emph{Avg} over all fourteen columns. Our
baselines are a vanilla transformer, a weight-shared Recursive transformer, and
biology-agnostic Mixture-of-Recursions (MoR) --- the adaptive-recursion backbone we build on
--- under both expert- and token-choice routing. Unless noted, $d{=}96$, $M{=}128$ markers,
$K{=}4$; the router capacity funnel, balancing coefficients, and full training hyperparameters
(held fixed across all datasets) are detailed in the supplementary material.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main}

Table~\ref{tab:main_cv5} reads the architecture as a ladder. The backbone we inherit from MoR
--- weight sharing and adaptive recursion --- delivers efficiency but \emph{not} accuracy:
Vanilla ($66.2$), Recursive ($66.4$) and biology-agnostic MoR ($64.4$--$67.1$~Avg) all lie
within run-to-run noise, so collapsing the $K$ independent layers into one shared block and
making depth adaptive preserves accuracy at $4\times$ fewer parameters ($75$K vs.\ $300$K) and
lower FLOPs. Our contribution --- the learned biological interaction graph --- is what moves
accuracy: bioMoR reaches the best Avg ($\mathbf{73.5\%}$), $+6.4$ points over the strongest MoR
configuration ($67.1\%$) at the \emph{identical} $75$K-parameter and FLOP budget. Because the
biology-agnostic and biology-guided models share architecture, parameters and compute, the
gain is attributable to biology-guided routing rather than to depth, capacity or added
computation, and the per-column highlights show it is broad rather than a single-suite effect. A gain this broad rather than concentrated on a few suites is itself diagnostic: a graph that
merely over-fit one benchmark would help only there, whereas a consistent lift across tissues and
assay types (Table~\ref{tab:main_cv5}) indicates the learned interaction structure captures
transcriptomic organisation that transfers. The gain is also graded --- largest on the harder
single-cell suites and the Reactome multi-omics cohorts and smallest where a task is already
near-saturated --- exactly the pattern expected if a biological inductive bias helps most when the
raw per-gene signal is weak and is redundant once the classes are trivially separable.

\paragraph{Which mechanism carries the biology?}
Figure~\ref{fig:injection} decomposes the learned graph into its three injection mechanisms ---
embedding smoothing (E), the depth router (R), and the attention bias (A) --- and their full
combination (ERA), in token mode on the Baron and Muraro pancreas atlases. Router- and
attention-bias-only injection are weak (Baron $R\,63.4$, $A\,65.9$; Muraro $R\,76.1$, $A\,78.1$),
whereas embedding smoothing carries most of the single-cell gain (Baron $80.1$; Muraro $82.8$).
Combining all three (ERA) is best on Baron ($85.3$, $+5.2$ over embedding alone) and matches
embedding on Muraro ($82.5$ vs.\ $82.8$), so the mechanisms are complementary rather than
redundant, with the largest gain where the task is hardest.

\paragraph{Biology helps only when the graph is learned, not imposed.}
Varying only how the router's gene--gene graph is \emph{formed}, a learned graph lifts mean
single-cell macro-F1 to $74.0\%$ --- $+11.3$ over a no-graph router ($62.7\%$) and above a
degree-matched random control ($62.2\%$) --- whereas a rigid hand-built biology prior
\emph{hurts}, dropping to $50.0\%$ ($-24.0$), below even the no-graph baseline, because a frozen
graph pins the router to an operating point it cannot leave. Warm-starting the learned graph
from biology leaves accuracy within noise ($74.6$ vs.\ $74.0$), so the model recovers the
useful structure from data on its own; forcing the prior to persist through an annealed anchor
only lowers accuracy further ($62.5$ vs.\ $72.1$ random-init; supplementary). Our finding is
thus mechanistic: biology helps as a \emph{learned}, end-to-end graph and is harmful as a fixed
prior.

\paragraph{Why learned, and why multiple sites.}
The ordering is mechanistically informative: a fixed prior applies every edge regardless of task
relevance and drags the router to a poor operating point (hence it sits below the no-graph
baseline), whereas the learned low-rank graph is optimised end-to-end and re-derives only the
discriminative subset of the co-expression/Reactome structure from data --- which is why a
biological warm-start is neutral (Figure~\ref{fig:injection}). The three mechanisms then apply
this graph for complementary functions: at the embedding it \emph{smooths} each token with its
neighbours (reducing variance in sparse single-cell measurements, carrying most of the single-cell
gain), in the attention it \emph{biases} each token toward its interaction neighbours during
refinement, and at the router it \emph{allocates} deeper recursion to tokens whose neighbourhood
marks them discriminative. Smoothing, biasing, and allocation are largely independent uses of the
structure, so their gains add, and the combined margin is largest where signal is weakest
(Baron, $+17.7$ over no graph).

\subsection{Interpretability}
\label{sec:qualitative}
bioMoR provides two forms of built-in interpretability that need no post-hoc attribution. First,
it reads out from a compact panel of $M{=}128$ marker/pathway tokens rather than the full gene set
(a marker-budget sweep, supplementary, shows a few dozen to a few hundred tokens recover most of
the full-gene accuracy), so the panel is an inspectable summary of the features the classifier uses.
Second, because the shared block is reused up to $K$ times at no parameter cost, each token's
recursion depth $d_m$ measures the computation --- and thus model reliance --- it receives, giving
an importance ordering a fixed panel cannot.
Figure~\ref{fig:pmdepth} makes this concrete on the pan-cancer PM cohort ($1{,}258$ Reactome
pathway tokens). Ranking pathways by mean recursion depth, each routing rule spends full depth on
only a small subset --- expert-choice keeps $379/1{,}258$ ($30\%$) of pathways to the full depth
$K{=}4$ and token-choice only $36$ ($3\%$), while the majority exit after one or two steps --- so
computation concentrates on a compact, inspectable set of pathways rather than being spread
uniformly. \emph{Which} pathways are kept is, however, specific to the routing rule: the
expert- and token-choice depth rankings do not coincide (their per-pathway mean depths are weakly
\emph{anti}-correlated, Spearman $-0.28$, with no overlap among the deepest-routed pathways), so the
depth ordering should be read within a routing mode rather than as a routing-invariant importance
score. The interpretable claim is therefore the sparse, rankable \emph{allocation of computation}
under a given router, not a single canonical pathway ranking; complete per-pathway depth tables for
both routings appear in the supplementary material.

The same economy holds in hardware-independent FLOPs. Making recursion adaptive lets most markers
exit early, cutting recursion FLOPs to as little as $0.42\times$ Vanilla (up to $58\%$ fewer) with
accuracy held within noise, so bioMoR sits on the accuracy--compute Pareto frontier on Muraro while

tokens and the block is shared across depth, quadratic attention is paid only on the marker panel
and depth is spent selectively, so computation tracks informativeness.

\subsection{Comparison to Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}
On the same 5-fold splits (Table~\ref{tab:baselines}), bioMoR outperforms strong non-transformer
classifiers --- mean macro-F1 $83.5$ vs.\ $67.0$ (linear ANOVA$\to$PCA$\to$logistic), $69.9$
(Random Forest) and $62.4$ (Nearest-Centroid) --- with the largest margin on the un-engineered
multi-omics input (PM: $86.8$ vs.\ $58.5$), where the classical read-out cannot exploit the
pathway structure bioMoR routes over. We also run \textbf{scBiGNN} \cite{scbignn2023}, a bilevel
graph network for single-cell data, head-to-head on the same folds: it is the strongest baseline
(mean $82.2$), beating all three classical methods, yet bioMoR still edges it on both cohorts (Lung
$80.3$ vs.\ $79.6$; PM $86.8$ vs.\ $84.8$) --- outperforming a dedicated graph network as well as
the classical read-outs. We do not claim uniform state of the art: on
near-linearly separable single-cell suites a full-feature linear model stays competitive with
bioMoR's compressed $M{=}128$-marker read-out, so we frame the value as efficiency,
interpretability, and the learned-graph mechanism rather than raw accuracy.

\section{Discussion}
bioMoR shows biological inductive bias and parameter-efficient recursion can be co-designed: weight
sharing and marker compression make the model small \emph{and} interpretable, and a label-free
interaction graph folds in without leaking labels or adding parameters. We credit a benefit only
where a graph separates from a degree-matched random control --- the \emph{learned} graph does
(best at both sites), the \emph{fixed} prior does not and can hurt --- while adaptive routing and
sharing cluster within noise on accuracy, so their benefit is efficiency and the interpretable
depth signal, not accuracy.

\paragraph{Limitations.} (i)~On some near-linearly separable single-cell suites a
full-feature linear pipeline stays competitive with bioMoR's compressed marker read-out, so
we do not claim uniform state-of-the-art accuracy; the gain is clearest on the un-engineered
multi-omics cohorts. (ii)~Efficiency claims are \emph{analytical} (parameters and
stack-level FLOPs), not yet matched-hardware wall-clock. (iii)~We compare head-to-head against
scBiGNN \cite{scbignn2023} (Table~\ref{tab:baselines}) but not against PATH \cite{howlader2026graph}
or gene-vocabulary foundation models, which require named-gene inputs the anonymised genomap features
do not expose; these remain natural next benchmarks. (iv)~Random-graph controls are degree-matched; extending
them to sparsity-matched smoothers and richer regulatory priors on larger atlases is where a
learned graph should bite hardest.

\section{Conclusion}
bioMoR unifies token compression, weight-shared adaptive recursion, and a biology-guided graph,
reaching $73.5\%$ average macro-F1 ($6$--$7$ points above an otherwise identical biology-free MoR,
vanilla, and recursive transformer) at a quarter of the transformer-stack parameters and up to
$58\%$ fewer recursion FLOPs. The graph must be \emph{learned} rather than imposed --- a learned
graph adds $+11.3$ single-cell macro-F1 over a no-graph router while a \emph{fixed} prior does not
separate from a random-graph control --- and composes cleanly with adaptive recursion when injected
through embedding smoothing, an attention bias, and the depth router.

\section{Broader Impact and Ethics Statement}
bioMoR targets cell-type annotation and bulk-omics subtyping with an auditable
marker-gene and recursion-depth signal. Its outputs are research tools, not clinical
decisions, and can be confidently wrong on tissues or populations absent from training, so
we report per-dataset error bars and degree-matched controls, and marker panels should be
checked for confounds (batch, donor, ambient RNA) before any biological conclusion. All
datasets are public and de-identified under their original licenses. Every run fits on a
single GPU and the full pipeline regenerates from one command.
